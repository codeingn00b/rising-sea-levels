Key Facts

  • Warming oceans expand in volume, contributing to long-term sea level rise.
  • Melting glaciers and ice sheets add water to the ocean, accelerating rise.
  • High-tide flooding is becoming more frequent in many coastal cities.
  • Saltwater intrusion threatens coastal aquifers and farmlands.
  • More people and infrastructure are concentrated in low-lying coastal zones.

Causes

Sea level rise results mainly from thermal expansion of seawater and ice melt from glaciers and ice sheets. Local land movement and ocean currents can increase or offset global averages.

Tip: Always distinguish between “global” and “local” sea level, considering regional land uplift or subsidence.

Effects

  • Flooding & erosion — higher water levels increase storm surge impacts.
  • Saltwater intrusion — contaminates freshwater sources and soils.
  • Ecosystem loss — wetlands and mangroves can drown if unable to migrate.
  • Infrastructure risk — coastal roads, ports, and homes face greater damage.
  • Economic costs — more insurance claims and relocation challenges.

Actions

Communities use three key approaches: Protect, Accommodate, Retreat.

Protect

Use sea walls, dunes, and mangroves to defend against flooding and erosion.

Accommodate

Elevate buildings, improve drainage, and adapt zoning codes to future water levels.

Retreat

Move vulnerable infrastructure inland and allow natural shoreline migration.

Adaptation Options

Case Study — United States

United States

Exposure: From Miami to New York and along the Gulf Coast, high-tide flooding has become far more frequent. The West Coast’s risk fluctuates with storms and El Niño cycles.

  • East & Gulf Coasts: Nuisance flooding days have multiplied since the 1960s.
  • West Coast: Storm-driven flooding depends on local elevation and subsidence.
  • Critical systems: Roads, ports, and wastewater plants in low-lying areas are most at risk.

Adaptation in practice: Cities combine protection (sea walls, mangroves), accommodation (elevating homes, drainage), and managed retreat (voluntary buyouts).

FAQ

Is sea level rise uniform worldwide?

No. Local factors like land movement and ocean currents make sea level rise uneven across regions.

How much could sea level rise by 2100?

Without major emission cuts, global sea level could rise 0.6–1.0 meters by 2100 according to NASA and IPCC projections.

What can individuals do?

Reduce carbon footprint, support wetland restoration, and encourage community adaptation planning.
